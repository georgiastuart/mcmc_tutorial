Outline

Motivation: Earth Properties

  • In exploration seismology, one aim is to find parameters profiles of the subsurface.
  • Finding the velocity profile can tell us about the materials in the subsurface.
  • Transmitted and reflected waves can tell us about velocities and interfaces between materials.

Seismic Data: Shot Gather

  • Shot gathers are receiver traces for one seismic source.
  • Traces are arranged by offset.
  • The y-axis is time.
  • Reflections show up in the traces.

The Goal: Inversion with Uncertainty Information

  • Deterministic inversion algorithms give no or limited uncertainty information.
  • Stochastic algorithms provide uncertainty information without imposing a certain shape on the distribution of the parameters.

A Solution for Trickier Distributions: Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)

  • MCMC methods provide a way to sample from a complicated probability distribution.
  • Distributions may be high dimensional.
  • Based off of constructing a Markov chain with the probability distribution as the equilibrium distribution.

The Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm

Random Walk Sampler

  • A random walk sampler produces points to evaluate using a random walk.
  • A random walk starts at the preceding point and takes a step in a random direction.
  • The steps in a random walk are correlated

Accepting and Rejecting

  • The Metropolis criterion determines whether a sample is accepted or rejected.
  • It's a ratio of the posterior values:
    • $$ \rho (\theta, \theta_n) = \min \left\{ \frac{P(\theta)}{P(\theta_n)}\right\} $$
  • The sample \(\theta\) is accepted or rejected with probability
    • $$ r(\theta, \theta_n) = \min\left\{1,\,\rho(\theta, \theta_n)\right\}$$

The Likelihood Function

  • The likelihood function measures the misfit between the simulated data and the observed data.
  • A common shape for the likelihood function is Gaussian: $$ P(d \vert \theta) = \exp \left(-\frac{\lVert d - \mathcal{F}(\theta)\rVert^2}{2\lVert d \rVert^2 \sigma^2}\right)$$ where \(\mathcal{F}(\theta)\) is the simulated data, \(d\) is the observed data, and \(\sigma\) is the precision parameter.
  • A Gaussian Likelihood does not mean the posterior will be Gaussian!

The Forward Problem

  • The forward problem produces your simulated data.
  • For the examples in this talk, the forward problem is the acoustic wave equation.
  • $$\frac{1}{c^2(x, z)} \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial t^2} - \nabla p = f$$ where \(p\) is pressure, \(t\) is time, \(c\) is velocity, and \(f\) is a seismic source.

What about uncertainty quantification?




We will use two main methods to visualize our uncertainty information:


  • Highest Posterior Density Intervals (HPDI)
  • Kernal Density Estimation (KDE)

Highest Posterior Density Intervals (HPDI)

  • A parameter falls within a credible interval with a certain probability.
  • Similar to confidence intervals in frequentist statistics.
  • A 95% highest posterior density (HPD) is the narrowest interval containing 95% of the posterior distribution.

Kernel Density Estimates (KDE)

  • Kernel density estimation (KDE) gives a probability distribution from a collection of samples.
  • Each sample forms a kernel which are then summed together.
  • $$\hat{f}(\theta) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} K_h(\theta - \theta_i)$$ where \(n\) is the number of samples and \(K_h\) is the kernel with bandwith \(h\).

The Problem with Random Walk Sampling

  • In theory, MCMC will converge to the posterior distribution.
  • In practice, methods based on Random Walk Sampling (RWS) can only handle a limited number of unkowns (<100 in my experience).
  • RWS produces samples that are highly correlated.

The Solution: Hybrid / Hamiltonian Monte Carlo

  • HMC uses Hamiltonian Mechanics and gradient information of the posterior distribution to draw samples that are less correlated.
  • Results in an algorithm that can handle higher dimensions and converges faster.

Hamiltonian Mechanics

    Reformulation of classical mechanics based on Hamilton's Equations, a system of differential equations.
    Predicts the same outcome as classical Newtonian Mechanics.

Hamilton's Equations

$$\frac{dq_i}{dt} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}$$ $$\frac{dp_i}{dt} = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}$$
  • \(H(q,\, p) = U(q) + K(p)\) is the total energy in the system.
  • \(U(q)\) is the potential energy.
  • \(U(p)\) is the kinetic energy.
  • \(q\) is position, \(p\) is momentum.
